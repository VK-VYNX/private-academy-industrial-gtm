# Module Field Guide

## Module Promise

This module gives operators a practical way to build industrial demand generation that creates trust, account movement, and qualified pipeline.

It is designed for markets where:

- sales cycles are long;
- buying committees are technical and conservative;
- trust is built through proof, repetition, and application specificity;
- demand volume is low but project value can be high;
- distribution often depends on experts, sales, associations, trade shows, partners, distributors, and referrals;
- success must be inspected through account movement, not vanity metrics.

## The Industrial Demand Generation System

The system has seven linked jobs:

1. Focus the market.
2. Understand the buying committee.
3. Map buyer informational needs.
4. Build useful content and proof assets.
5. Distribute through trusted channels.
6. Route signals by fit, demand state, role, and evidence.
7. Inspect movement every week.

If one job is missing, the system weakens. If all seven work together, demand generation becomes an operating rhythm rather than a campaign calendar.

## Core Language

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Content demand | the buyer wants to understand a problem, risk, trend, process, or requirement |
| Solution demand | the buyer is comparing ways to solve the problem |
| Vendor demand | the buyer is evaluating suppliers, proof, risk, price, implementation, or procurement |
| MOIN | Map Of Informational Needs: buyer questions by role and demand state |
| Future Pipeline | high-fit account with engagement or awareness but unconfirmed need |
| Active Focus | high-fit account with evidence of need, timing, stakeholder, or relationship path |
| Buyer enablement | proof assets that help the champion and committee buy internally |
| Signal routing | interpreting evidence before deciding nurture, research, activation, opportunity review, or disqualification |

## Integrative Questions

Ask these in every unit:

- How does this affect sales?
- What signal would prove this is working?
- What would RevOps need to track?
- What content would help the buyer move forward?
- What would an AI agent likely get wrong here?
- What would make this fail in an industrial company?

## What Good Looks Like

A good Module 1 output:

- names a narrow ICP;
- explains why the category matters now;
- maps at least five buying committee roles;
- separates content, solution, and vendor demand;
- builds content from buyer questions, not campaign ideas;
- distributes through trusted industrial channels;
- routes signals with owner, SLA, and false-positive risk;
- measures account progression and committee coverage;
- includes stop, repair, and scale criteria.

## What Bad Looks Like

Reject work that:

- treats all engagement as buying intent;
- makes content volume the strategy;
- hides behind broad TAM language;
- depends only on company-page posting;
- asks sales to follow up on weak signals;
- ignores procurement, finance, quality, maintenance, or leadership;
- reports impressions, likes, raw traffic, or MQLs as primary success;
- accepts AI output without human correction.

## Field Completion Standard

You are done only when a revenue leader can inspect your 90-day pilot and answer:

1. Who are we targeting?
2. Why these accounts?
3. What buyer questions will we answer?
4. How will the right buyers see the answers?
5. What signals will we capture?
6. Who acts on those signals?
7. What does sales do next?
8. What does RevOps track?
9. What will we review weekly?
10. What would make us stop, repair, or scale?
