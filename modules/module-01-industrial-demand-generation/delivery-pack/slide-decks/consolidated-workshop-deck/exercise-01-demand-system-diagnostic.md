# Exercise 01 - Demand System Diagnostic Worksheet

Use this worksheet with Slide 07 of the Industrial Demand Generation workshop.

This exercise helps you diagnose the current demand generation system for one industrial product or product category. You are not expected to invent expert terminology. For each layer, choose the statement that best matches your current reality.

## Exercise Outcome

By the end of this exercise, you will have:

- Six operating-layer scores;
- A clear view of the weakest parts of the current demand system;
- Three priority repairs to carry into the rest of the workshop.

The output becomes the first section of your Industrial Demand Generation strategy.

## How To Complete This Diagnostic

1. Read the five statements for each layer.
2. Choose the one statement that is closest to your current reality.
3. Write the score in the score summary table.
4. Use evidence, not optimism. If two statements feel possible, choose the lower score unless your team can point to current proof.
5. Select the three weakest layers as repair priorities.

Do not spend time searching for perfect wording. The purpose is to quickly reveal where the demand system is unclear, missing, or not yet operational.

## Scoring Ladder

| Score | Status | Plain Meaning |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | Missing Or Mostly Accidental | This layer is not intentionally designed. It depends on luck, memory, individual effort, or scattered activity. |
| 2 | Informal Or Inconsistent | The layer exists in conversation, but it is not clear enough to guide repeatable GTM action. |
| 3 | Partially Defined | The layer is documented or partly understood, but it is not yet used consistently by marketing, sales, SMEs, and RevOps. |
| 4 | Clear And Usable | The layer is clear enough to guide current GTM work, sales action, content decisions, and review conversations. |
| 5 | Managed And Improving | The layer is actively reviewed, corrected, and improved from account movement evidence. |

## Layer 1 - Market Focus

Market Focus answers: Where are we trying to create movement in the next 90 days?

| Score | Choose This Statement If It Best Matches Your Current Reality |
|---:|---|
| 1 | We pursue almost any company that could buy. Sales, marketing, content, and events are spread across many sectors, use cases, or regions. |
| 2 | We have preferred sectors or regions, but there is no clear 90-day focus that guides content, distribution, sales follow-up, and exclusions. |
| 3 | We have a target segment for the next 90 days, but the trigger, urgency, geography, use case, or exclusion logic is still unclear. |
| 4 | We have a clear 90-day segment, trigger, geography or use case, and exclusion rules. Most activity can be judged against that focus. |
| 5 | Focus is actively managed. Weak-fit demand is rejected or downgraded, and the weekly review can explain which target accounts moved and why. |

## Layer 2 - ICP And Disqualification

ICP and Disqualification answer: Which accounts deserve effort, and which accounts should be rejected, downgraded, or nurtured?

| Score | Choose This Statement If It Best Matches Your Current Reality |
|---:|---|
| 1 | We can describe possible buyers, but we cannot reliably separate high-fit accounts from weak-fit accounts before sales time is spent. |
| 2 | Experienced salespeople know fit informally, but the criteria are person-dependent and not visible in CRM, account lists, or working boards. |
| 3 | We have basic ICP criteria, but disqualification rules are missing, too soft, or not used consistently. |

## Layer 3 - Demand Creation And Buyer Education

Demand Creation answers: Are we helping buyers understand the problem, risk, approach, proof, and decision criteria before they ask for price?

| Score | Choose This Statement If It Best Matches Your Current Reality |
|---:|---|
| 1 | Our materials are mostly product brochures, catalog pages, exhibition posts, capability decks, or price-led sales material. |
| 2 | We have some educational content, but it is scattered and not tied to specific buyer questions, roles, or demand states. |
| 3 | We cover important topics, but assets are not clearly mapped to content demand, solution demand, and vendor demand. |
| 4 | We have credible assets that answer buyer questions, reduce risk, support sales use, and carry proof for the main demand states. |
| 5 | Content is managed as a proof system. Assets are built from buyer questions, reviewed with sales and SMEs, used in conversations, and improved from signal evidence. |

## Layer 4 - Trusted Distribution

Trusted Distribution answers: How will useful insight reach the right industrial buyers through paths they actually trust?

| Score | Choose This Statement If It Best Matches Your Current Reality |
|---:|---|
| 1 | We publish or send materials, but there is no repeatable plan for getting insight into trusted industrial paths. |
| 2 | Distribution depends on ad hoc posts, event follow-up, or individual salespeople. Some content is shared, but ownership and cadence are unclear. |
| 3 | We have selected channels, but the trust reason, target role, follow-up rule, and account signal are not defined for each channel. |
| 4 | Distribution uses trusted paths such as sales, SMEs, events, associations, distributors, partners, owned channels, search, or paid amplification with clear owners. |
| 5 | Distribution is inspected weekly by account movement, role coverage, sales use, and signal quality. Channels are repaired or stopped when they do not create movement. |

## Layer 5 - Signal Capture And Routing

Signal Capture and Routing answer: What does buyer activity mean, and what should happen next?

| Score | Choose This Statement If It Best Matches Your Current Reality |
|---:|---|
| 1 | Engagement is counted, but signals are not interpreted. Forms, clicks, event scans, RFQs, and conversations are treated too similarly. |
| 2 | Sales receives leads or inquiries with limited context. Fit, role, demand state, source, and signal strength are usually unclear. |
| 3 | We classify some signals, but routing rules are incomplete, inconsistent, or not connected to owner and SLA decisions. |
| 4 | Signals are routed by account fit, demand state, role, source, strength, recency, owner, SLA, and next action. |
| 5 | Signal routing is an operating discipline. False positives are reviewed, sales action is matched to evidence, and RevOps can show account progression. |

## Layer 6 - Cadence And Ownership

Cadence and Ownership answer: Who reviews movement, makes decisions, and repairs the system every week?

| Score | Choose This Statement If It Best Matches Your Current Reality |
|---:|---|
| 1 | Marketing reports activity, sales follows up separately, and there is no regular meeting to repair the demand system. |
| 2 | We have meetings or reports, but they focus mostly on lead volume, campaign activity, website traffic, or anecdotal sales feedback. |
| 3 | We review some account and pipeline information, but ownership, repair decisions, and next actions are inconsistent. |
| 4 | A weekly cadence reviews target-account movement, signal quality, proof gaps, sales use, stuck accounts, owners, and commitments. |
| 5 | Cadence changes behavior. Every week the team stops, repairs, or reinforces specific parts of ICP, content, distribution, routing, sales action, and RevOps visibility. |

## Interpreting The Scores

Use the score to understand the current operating condition.

| Score Range | Diagnosis | What It Means |
|---:|---|---|
| 1-2 | Repair Required | This layer is likely blocking account movement or wasting commercial effort. |
| 3 | Operational Gap | This layer exists, but it is not reliable enough for a serious 90-day pilot. |
| 4 | Usable Foundation | This layer can support the pilot, but should still be reviewed for precision. |
| 5 | Managed Strength | This layer is likely a strength. Protect it and use it to support weaker layers. |

Do not average away the problem. A single score of 1 or 2 can weaken the full system even if other layers look healthy.

## Selecting Three Repair Priorities

Choose three repair priorities using this rule:

1. Mark every layer scored 1 or 2 as a repair candidate.
2. If more than three layers are scored 1 or 2, choose the three that most directly block account movement.
3. If several layers are tied, prioritize in this order:
   - Market Focus or ICP if weak-fit demand is consuming effort;
   - Demand Creation or Trusted Distribution if buyers are not being educated or reached through trusted paths;
   - Signal Capture or Cadence if activity exists but the team does not know what to do next.
4. Your final repair set should include:
   - One Focus or ICP repair;
   - One Demand Creation or Distribution repair;
   - One Signal, Owner, or Cadence repair.

## Repair Menu

Use this menu to turn low scores into practical repairs.

| Weak Layer | Strong Repair Wording |
|---|---|
| Market Focus | Select one 90-day segment, one trigger, one geography or use case, and clear exclusions. |
| ICP And Disqualification | Define fit criteria and rejection rules before sales or SME time is spent. |
| Demand Creation And Buyer Education | Build one proof-backed asset that answers a real buyer question for one role and one demand state. |
| Trusted Distribution | Assign one trusted channel, owner, cadence, target role, expected signal, and follow-up rule. |
| Signal Capture And Routing | Define how each signal will be classified by fit, demand state, role, source, strength, owner, SLA, and next action. |
| Cadence And Ownership | Run a weekly movement review that creates decisions, commitments, and system repairs. |

## Quality Check

Before moving to the next workshop section, confirm:

- You selected one score per layer;
- Your scores reflect current evidence, not aspiration;
- You selected three repair priorities;
- At least one repair improves focus, ICP, or disqualification;
- At least one repair improves buyer education, proof, or distribution;
- At least one repair improves signal routing, ownership, or cadence;
- No repair is written as "make more content," "get more leads," or "run more campaigns."
