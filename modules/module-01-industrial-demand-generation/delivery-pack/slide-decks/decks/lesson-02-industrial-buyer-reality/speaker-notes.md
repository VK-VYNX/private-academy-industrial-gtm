## Instructor Use

Use these notes as the instructor depth layer. The projected slides are intentionally concise so the instructor can teach, diagnose, and run artifact production without turning the deck into a handout.

## Slide 01: Industrial Buyer Reality

- Open by naming this as 20 min of operating work, not a lecture.
- Anchor the room on the lesson output: Buyer reality and committee map.
- Remind participants to keep one manufacturing or export category consistent through the workshop.

## Slide 02: Lesson Promise

- Map the real industrial buying committee, identify what each role needs to believe, and define the proof assets needed to move the account.
- The required output is: A committee map with concerns, blockers, proof needs, enablement assets, and owners.
- Do not move on until learners know what they will submit at the end of the lesson.

## Slide 03: Why This Matters In Industrial Markets

- Industrial buyers rarely buy alone.
- The visible contact may like the solution while quality, finance, procurement, maintenance, or leadership remains unconvinced.
- Most deal stalls are missing internal proof, not missing follow-up.
- Buyer enablement matters because the champion sells internally when the supplier is absent.
- Instructor move: ask for a current example where the team is measuring activity while the account has not actually moved.

## Slide 04: Core Framework

- Buyer enablement asks what the account must believe.
- Visible champion
- Decision maker
- Technical evaluator
- Production or operations
- Quality, validation, or compliance
- Maintenance and service
- Procurement, finance, leadership, consultants, distributors
- Visual companion: 03-industrial-buyer-committee-map.svg.

## Slide 05: Mental Model

- Sales enablement and buyer enablement are different jobs.
- Seller focus: What should sales say in the meeting?
- Buyer focus: What must the champion repeat internally?
- Seller asset: A better pitch deck.
- Buyer asset: Role-specific proof, risk reduction, and internal case logic.
- Ask the room which part would break first in their current operating system.

## Slide 06: Anonymized Industrial Example

- Composite example: Composite regulated packaging machinery supplier.
- A production head at a pharma packaging plant wants line efficiency and faster changeover.
- Sales sends a proposal and follows up. The deal stalls.
- Hidden concerns remain across quality, engineering, finance, procurement, and leadership.
- The repair is validation proof, implementation roadmap, lifecycle cost logic, vendor comparison, and cost-of-delay case.
- Keep the example anonymized and category-level. Do not invite named-company comparisons.

## Slide 07: Diagnostic Questions

- Who must believe what before this account can move?
- Who is the visible champion?
- Which roles can block the decision?
- Which roles are invisible today?
- What does each role fear?
- What proof can the champion circulate internally?
- What committee coverage should RevOps inspect?
- Use the prompts as a rapid room diagnostic before the artifact exercise.

## Slide 08: Exercise Setup

- Buying committee map: 6 min map, 4 min proof-gap review.
- List at least five roles for the selected category.
- For each role, write current belief, required belief, proof needed, asset, and owner.
- Mark which roles are mapped, engaged, enabled, missing, or blockers.
- Expected artifact: Buyer reality and committee map.

## Slide 09: Group Critique

- Use the critique loop: learner presents, peers name strength, peers identify one false assumption or missing owner, then the group repairs the artifact.
- Does the map separate roles instead of compressing them into one persona?
- Does it include blockers, not only supporters?
- Does each critical role have a circulatable proof asset?
- Does it include procurement and finance before late-stage negotiation?
- Does it account for consultants, distributors, or project advisors where relevant?

## Slide 10: Structured Drafting Drill

- Use this as the structured drafting brief.
- For the selected industrial category, map buying committee roles, role concerns, proof needs, likely blocker hypotheses, buyer-circulatable enablement assets, and unknowns that require sales or SME validation. Do not invent a named-company case.
- Require the draft to list assumptions and unknowns before learners accept any recommendation.
- Includes operational, technical, commercial, quality, finance, procurement, leadership, and ecosystem roles where relevant
- States real decision risk, not generic persona pain
- Names specific proof or evidence
- Separates fact, assumption, and unknown

## Slide 11: Human Quality Control

- The correction standard is operational judgment, not cosmetic polish.
- Reject neat personas that do not match the category.
- Reject maps that ignore quality, maintenance, procurement, finance, or leadership.
- Reject generic concerns like cost without the underlying risk.
- Reject sales decks when the buyer needs role-specific proof.
- Reject named-company examples presented as evidence.
- Instructor move: ask what field evidence, SME proof, or sales reality is missing.

## Slide 12: RevOps Translation

- Committee coverage must be visible account by account.
- Known buying role
- Role coverage status
- Proof asset shared
- Committee gap
- Champion strength
- Blocker risk
- Next committee action
- If this cannot be represented in a CRM field, board, owner model, or temporary working table, it is not operational yet.

## Slide 13: Failure Modes / Anti-Patterns

- Use these anti-patterns to normalize rejection of weak work.
- One-person persona: All content targets the decision maker -> Map real roles and proof needs
- Late procurement surprise: Procurement appears only at quote stage -> Create vendor comparison logic earlier
- Technical proof gap: Interest stalls after demo -> Build application, validation, integration, or test proof
- Champion unsupported: Main contact cannot sell internally -> Give internal case and objection handler

## Slide 14: Final Artifact

- Learners submit: Buyer reality and committee map.
- At least five committee roles
- Current and required belief for each role
- One proof need per critical role
- One enablement asset per blocker or missing role
- Owner for each asset or delivery action
- Committee coverage gaps for RevOps
- Exit gate: the artifact must change sales behavior, RevOps tracking, content decisions, or weekly cadence.

## Slide 15: Transition

- The committee map exposes whose belief must change. The next lesson decides which accounts deserve that much operating effort.
- Next: Lesson 03 - ICP, Segments, And Demand Focus.
- Ask learners what artifact they are carrying forward before opening the next deck.
