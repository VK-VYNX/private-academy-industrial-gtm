## Instructor Use

Use these notes as the instructor depth layer. The projected slides are intentionally concise so the instructor can teach, diagnose, and run artifact production without turning the deck into a handout.

## Slide 01: Signal Routing And Demand Capture

- Open by naming this as 24 min of operating work, not a lecture.
- Anchor the room on the lesson output: Signal-routing table with owner, SLA, next action, and false-positive risk.
- Remind participants to keep one manufacturing or export category consistent through the workshop.

## Slide 02: Lesson Promise

- Classify industrial demand signals by account fit, demand state, buying role, and evidence strength, then route each signal to the right next action.
- The required output is: A signal-routing table with owner, SLA, next action, false-positive risk, and opportunity review criteria.
- Do not move on until learners know what they will submit at the end of the lesson.

## Slide 03: Why This Matters In Industrial Markets

- The most expensive demand generation error is treating every signal as buying intent.
- Industrial engagement can mean learning, comparison, distributor checking, procurement research, or active supplier evaluation.
- Signals are useful only after interpretation.
- Routing protects sales from noise while ensuring strong vendor-demand evidence gets acted on quickly.
- Instructor move: ask for a current example where the team is measuring activity while the account has not actually moved.

## Slide 04: Core Framework

- Use four filters before assigning action.
- Account fit
- Demand state
- Buying committee role
- Evidence strength
- Then choose education, research, mapping, enablement, activation, opportunity review, or disqualification
- Visual companion: 08-signal-routing-and-account-progression.svg.

## Slide 05: Mental Model

- Account progression is not a straight jump to opportunity.
- Cluster ICP: Fits target segment; need unknown.
- Future Pipeline: Engagement or awareness; need unconfirmed.
- Active Focus: Need, trigger, stakeholder, timing, or relationship evidence.
- Opportunity: Declared requirement, value logic, champion, and next step.
- Ask the room which part would break first in their current operating system.

## Slide 06: Anonymized Industrial Example

- Composite example: Composite calibration and validation services provider.
- A quality coordinator reads one audit-readiness post. That is weak content demand.
- The same account returns to a calibration interval guide. Research the account and audit timing.
- A validation head attends a webinar and asks about documentation. Map committee and send validation proof.
- A plant head requests an audit readiness review. Strong vendor demand triggers Active Focus and sales action.
- Keep the example anonymized and category-level. Do not invite named-company comparisons.

## Slide 07: Diagnostic Questions

- Which signals deserve education, research, activation, opportunity review, or disqualification?
- What is the account fit?
- What demand state does the signal represent?
- Which role created the signal?
- Is it weak, moderate, or strong?
- What is the false-positive risk?
- What is the SLA?
- Use the prompts as a rapid room diagnostic before the artifact exercise.

## Slide 08: Exercise Setup

- Classify and route five signals: 8 min routing table, 5 min opportunity criteria.
- Classify each signal by source, account fit, role, demand state, strength, owner, SLA, next action, and false-positive risk.
- Define opportunity review evidence: ICP fit, named stakeholder, need, value, demand state, next step, and committee risk.
- Downgrade weak or low-fit signals before sales receives them.
- Expected artifact: Signal-routing table with owner, SLA, next action, and false-positive risk.

## Slide 09: Group Critique

- Use the critique loop: learner presents, peers name strength, peers identify one false assumption or missing owner, then the group repairs the artifact.
- Name one signal being over-routed to sales.
- Name one signal that deserves faster action.
- Name one signal that requires committee mapping.
- Name one signal that should be disqualified or low-touch nurtured.
- Name one missing field that would make routing unreliable.

## Slide 10: Structured Drafting Drill

- Use this as the structured drafting brief.
- Classify the signal table. For each signal, assign demand state, strength, progression state, owner, SLA, next action, false-positive concern, and missing evidence. Explain why the signal is or is not buying intent. Do not route weak engagement as an opportunity.
- Require the draft to list assumptions and unknowns before learners accept any recommendation.
- Demand state with rationale
- Signal strength and progression state
- Owner and SLA
- Specific next action
- False-positive concern
- Missing evidence before escalation

## Slide 11: Human Quality Control

- The correction standard is operational judgment, not cosmetic polish.
- Reject engagement equals intent.
- Reject skipped ICP fit checks.
- Reject routing that ignores buying role.
- Reject outreach without relevance.
- Reject weak signals escalated as opportunities.
- Reject strong vendor-demand signals left in nurture.
- Instructor move: ask what field evidence, SME proof, or sales reality is missing.

## Slide 12: RevOps Translation

- Signal routing needs a signal object or working board.
- Signal date
- Account
- Contact or role
- Signal source
- Asset or context
- Demand state and strength
- Fit and progression state
- Owner, SLA, next action
- False-positive risk
- If this cannot be represented in a CRM field, board, owner model, or temporary working table, it is not operational yet.

## Slide 13: Failure Modes / Anti-Patterns

- Use these anti-patterns to normalize rejection of weak work.
- Intent inflation: Every click becomes a lead -> Route by fit, state, role, and strength
- Weak sales handoff: Sales receives names without context -> Provide signal reason and next action
- Signal decay: Strong signal sits untouched -> Define owner and SLA
- CRM blindness: Signals live in chats or notes -> Create signal object or shared board

## Slide 14: Final Artifact

- Learners submit: Signal-routing table with owner, SLA, next action, and false-positive risk.
- Weak, moderate, and strong signal examples
- Content, solution, and vendor demand
- Source class and buying role
- Owner and SLA
- Next action
- False-positive risk
- Opportunity review criteria
- Exit gate: the artifact must change sales behavior, RevOps tracking, content decisions, or weekly cadence.

## Slide 15: Transition

- Routing creates disciplined next actions. The final lesson turns those actions into weekly governance and a 90-day pilot decision.
