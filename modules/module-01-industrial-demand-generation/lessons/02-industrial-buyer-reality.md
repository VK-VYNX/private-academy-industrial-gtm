## Concept

Industrial buyers rarely buy alone.

The visible contact may be a production head, quality head, procurement manager, distributor, consultant, plant engineer, founder, or owner. But the deal usually depends on a larger group of people who care about different risks:

- will it work technically?
- will it disrupt production?
- will it pass audit or validation?
- will maintenance support it?
- will procurement approve the supplier?
- will finance accept the payback logic?
- will leadership believe the timing is urgent?
- will the champion look credible internally?

Demand generation must therefore educate a committee before sales asks for a decision. If only one role is educated, the account can look interested but still stall.

## Sales Enablement Versus Buyer Enablement

Sales enablement helps the seller present.

Buyer enablement helps the buyer buy.

In industrial demand generation, buyer enablement is more important than a polished pitch deck because the champion often has to sell internally when the supplier is not in the room.

| Seller-Focused Thinking | Buyer-Enablement Thinking |
|---|---|
| What should sales say? | What must the buyer believe and repeat internally? |
| What deck will persuade the contact? | What proof can the champion circulate to other roles? |
| How do we get a meeting? | How do we help the account reduce decision risk? |
| How do we handle objections live? | How do we equip the buyer before objections appear? |
| How do we close faster? | What internal consensus is missing? |

## Industrial Buying Committee Roles

Use this committee map as a starting point. Adapt it by category.

| Role | Primary Concern | Proof Needed | Common Blocker |
|---|---|---|---|
| Champion | Will this solve my problem and make me credible? | one-page case, objection handler, internal summary | fear of pushing unsupported change |
| Decision maker | Is this worth funding now? | cost of inaction, executive case, ROI logic | unclear urgency |
| Technical evaluator | Will it work in our environment? | specs, validation, test data, integration note | performance uncertainty |
| Production | Will it disrupt throughput or operators? | implementation plan, downtime estimate, operator impact | disruption risk |
| Quality | Will it improve quality or pass audit? | validation checklist, compliance note, inspection data | audit or documentation anxiety |
| Maintenance | Will it be reliable and serviceable? | service plan, spares, uptime proof | support burden |
| Procurement | Is the vendor credible and commercially safe? | vendor comparison, terms, references, risk checklist | supplier approval risk |
| Finance | What is the payback and downside risk? | TCO, payback, sensitivity analysis | budget uncertainty |
| Consultant or distributor | Can I recommend this safely? | technical dossier, delivery constraints, proof record | reputation risk |

## Why Deals Stall

Most industrial deal stalls are not caused by lack of interest. They are caused by missing internal proof.

Common stall patterns:

- production likes the solution but quality lacks validation proof;
- engineering likes the specs but finance has no payback story;
- a champion likes the supplier but procurement needs comparison logic;
- leadership likes the business case but maintenance fears service burden;
- an event conversation was strong but no one mapped the committee after the show;
- a distributor reports "interest" but cannot identify the role, timing, or trigger.

The correction is not more follow-up. The correction is committee enablement.

## Industrial Example

A composite regulated packaging machinery supplier receives serious interest from a production head at a pharma packaging plant. The production head wants line efficiency and faster changeover. Sales creates a proposal and follows up repeatedly. The deal stalls.

The hidden committee reality:

| Role | Hidden Concern | Missing Proof |
|---|---|---|
| Production | changeover gain must not create operator confusion | changeover workflow and training plan |
| Quality | GMP, validation, and documentation must be credible | validation checklist and compliance dossier |
| Engineering | integration and utility requirements must be clear | installation and integration note |
| Finance | payback cannot depend only on optimistic utilization | conservative payback model |
| Procurement | supplier must be comparable and defensible | vendor comparison framework |
| Leadership | project timing must be worth prioritizing | cost of delay and expansion logic |

The demand generation repair is to build content and proof before the decision is active:

- educational content on line efficiency and changeover loss;
- validation-readiness note for quality teams;
- implementation roadmap for production and engineering;
- lifecycle cost model for finance;
- procurement comparison checklist;
- executive case explaining why the project matters now.

## Diagnostic Question

Who must believe what before this account can move?

Use these follow-up questions:

- Who is the visible champion?
- Which roles can block the decision?
- Which roles are currently invisible?
- What does each role fear?
- What proof already exists?
- What proof is missing?
- What asset can the champion circulate internally?
- What would sales need to know before activation?
- What would RevOps need to track as committee coverage?

## Group Critique

The group reviews whether the map:

- separates committee roles instead of compressing them into one persona;
- includes blockers, not only supporters;
- names proof that can be circulated internally;
- identifies where sales needs marketing, RevOps, technical, or leadership support;
- includes procurement and finance before late-stage negotiation;
- accounts for consultants, distributors, associations, or EPC/project advisors where relevant.

The group should challenge any answer that says "the decision maker is the owner" without mapping technical, operational, quality, financial, and procurement risk.

## AI-Assisted Attempt

Ask AI to generate role concerns and proof needs for the selected category.

Required AI output:

| Output | Required Standard |
|---|---|
| Committee roles | include operational, technical, commercial, quality, finance, procurement, leadership, and ecosystem roles where relevant |
| Role concern | state the real decision risk, not a generic persona pain |
| Required proof | name a specific asset or evidence type |
| Blocker hypothesis | explain how the deal can stall |
| Enablement recommendation | suggest a buyer-circulatable asset |
| Unknowns | list facts that require sales or SME validation |

## Human Correction

Humans must repair AI output when it:

- invents a neat persona that does not match the category;
- ignores quality, maintenance, procurement, finance, or leadership;
- assumes one champion can decide alone;
- lists generic concerns like "cost" without explaining the underlying risk;
- recommends a sales deck where the buyer needs role-specific proof;
- misses distributors, consultants, project advisors, or regulatory stakeholders;
- treats a named-company example as real evidence.

## RevOps Translation

Committee mapping must become inspectable. RevOps should be able to answer:

- how many target accounts have at least one named stakeholder;
- how many have more than one role engaged;
- which roles are missing by account;
- which roles consumed content;
- which proof assets were shared;
- which accounts are single-threaded;
- which blocker is unresolved;
- which owner is responsible for the next role or proof gap.

Minimum fields:

| Field | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Known buying role | identifies the role of each contact or stakeholder |
| Role coverage status | unknown, mapped, engaged, enabled, blocker |
| Proof asset shared | records role-specific enablement |
| Committee gap | names the missing role or risk |
| Champion strength | weak, moderate, strong |
| Blocker risk | technical, quality, production, procurement, finance, leadership, channel |
| Next committee action | map, educate, enable, activate, escalate |

## Failure Modes

| Failure Mode | What It Looks Like | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| One-person persona | all content is written for "decision maker" | map real roles and proof needs |
| Late procurement surprise | procurement appears only at quote stage | create vendor comparison and approval logic earlier |
| Technical proof gap | interest stalls after demo or call | build application notes, validation, integration, or test proof |
| Champion unsupported | main contact cannot sell internally | give one-page internal case and objection handler |
| Quality ignored | compliance or audit concerns appear late | build validation and documentation proof early |
| Finance ignored | ROI is assumed, not evidenced | create conservative TCO or payback logic |
| Channel blind spot | distributor or consultant influence is invisible | map ecosystem roles and routing rules |

## Final Artifact

Submit a buying committee map with:

- at least five committee roles;
- current belief and required belief for each role;
- one proof need per critical role;
- one buyer-enablement asset per blocker or missing role;
- owner for building or delivering each asset;
- committee coverage gaps RevOps must track.

## Completion Standard

This unit is complete only when the learner can explain:

- who the visible champion is;
- who can block the decision;
- what each role needs to believe;
- what proof exists;
- what proof must be built;
- how sales should use the proof;
- how RevOps will inspect committee coverage.

If the map only describes personas and not decision risk, it fails.
