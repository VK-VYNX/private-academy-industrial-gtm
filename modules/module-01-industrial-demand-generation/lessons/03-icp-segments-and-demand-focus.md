## Concept

Industrial demand generation begins with a hard choice: where will the system try to create movement?

Many industrial companies say they can serve everyone:

- all manufacturers;
- all exporters;
- all OEMs;
- all plants needing automation;
- all buyers needing quality improvement;
- all companies attending an exhibition.

That is not a demand focus. It is an addressable market fantasy.

A real industrial ICP defines where the company has the right combination of:

- operational problem;
- trigger or timing;
- technical fit;
- commercial value;
- buying committee access;
- proof strength;
- ability to distribute insight;
- ability to win or learn within the pilot window.

The purpose of ICP is not to make a nice description. The purpose is to protect sales, marketing, RevOps, technical teams, and leadership from scattered effort.

## ICP In Industrial Terms

An industrial ICP should include more than firmographics.

| ICP Dimension | What It Means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Sector or category | the industrial context where the problem exists | regulated pharma packaging, food processing, electrical equipment |
| Account type | the kind of company or plant | export-oriented manufacturer, OEM, EPC, distributor-led buyer |
| Trigger | the reason the account may move now | audit, expansion, quality issue, replacement cycle, new product line |
| Technical fit | conditions that make the solution relevant | process type, capacity, compliance level, material, application |
| Commercial fit | deal value, margin, repeat potential, service potential | high-value project, recurring calibration, repeat consumables |
| Buying process | direct, tender-led, consultant-led, distributor-led, owner-led | consultant specification or technical audit |
| Access path | how trust can reach the account | association, event, distributor, sales relationship, expert content |
| Disqualification | what makes the account not worth pilot effort | low value, weak trigger, wrong process, locked supplier, no committee access |

## Why Focus Is Revenue Discipline

Industrial demand generation is resource-intensive. Good content needs SME input. Distribution requires relationships. Sales activation requires context. Buyer enablement requires proof. RevOps needs fields and cadence.

If the ICP is too broad:

- content becomes generic;
- distribution becomes channel-first instead of buyer-first;
- sales cannot tell which accounts deserve action;
- weak-fit engagement pollutes signal routing;
- RevOps dashboards report noise;
- leadership asks to scale before the system has proof.

Focus makes the engine learn faster.

## Segment Selection Logic

Do not choose the biggest segment automatically. Choose the segment where the pilot can create meaningful account movement.

Use these criteria:

| Criterion | Question | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Pain intensity | Is there a problem worth solving now? | weak pain creates passive education but not movement |
| Trigger visibility | Can we detect expansion, audit, defect, replacement, tender, or project timing? | invisible timing makes activation speculative |
| Technical fit | Can we credibly solve the problem with proof? | poor fit damages trust |
| Commercial value | Is the account worth sales and SME effort? | low value cannot support heavy GTM effort |
| Committee complexity | Can we map and enable the real buying committee? | unknown committee creates stalled deals |
| Access path | Can we reach buyers through trusted channels? | no access means content stays theoretical |
| Sales credibility | Can sales hold a relevant conversation? | weak sales relevance kills activation |
| Proof availability | Do we have or can we build evidence quickly? | proof gaps slow pilot learning |

## Industrial Example

A composite contract manufacturing and precision fabrication supplier says its market is "all OEMs that outsource manufacturing."

That is too broad for demand generation. It creates broad content, weak distribution, and poor account routing.

Three candidate segments:

| Segment | Fit Evidence | Weakness |
|---|---|---|
| all OEMs needing fabrication | large market | too broad, no shared trigger, low content specificity |
| export-oriented equipment OEMs with tolerance and documentation requirements | strong repeat potential, quality role involved, proof matters | requires sharper technical content |
| one-off local fabrication jobs | easy inbound demand | low value, price pressure, weak strategic fit |

The sharper ICP:

```text
For the next 90 days, focus on export-oriented equipment OEMs that need medium-to-high complexity fabricated components with tolerance control, documentation, and repeat production potential. Prioritize accounts showing new product, vendor diversification, quality issue, or capacity bottleneck triggers. Exclude one-off fabrication jobs, accounts with no repeat potential, and accounts where procurement only wants lowest-price comparison.
```

This focus creates better operating decisions:

- content can explain manufacturability, tolerance control, inspection, and documentation risk;
- sales can pursue fewer accounts with more relevant insight;
- RevOps can track trigger evidence and quality role coverage;
- disqualification can protect the team from price-shopping work;
- the capstone can produce a realistic 90-day pilot.

## Diagnostic Question

Which accounts are valuable enough, specific enough, and reachable enough to justify demand generation effort?

Use these prompts:

- What accounts are expensive enough to deserve human attention?
- What trigger makes the problem current?
- Which roles must be educated?
- What proof do we already have?
- What accounts should we stop pursuing?
- What account types create low-quality RFQs?
- Where does sales currently waste time?
- What segment can produce learning in 90 days?

## Disqualification Rules

Every ICP must include rejection rules. A pilot without disqualification is not focused.

Use this table:

| Disqualification Category | Example Rule | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Low value | expected value below resource threshold | protects sales and SME time |
| Weak technical fit | wrong process, application, material, capacity, compliance level | prevents failed proof claims |
| No trigger | no expansion, audit, quality issue, replacement, or project timing | avoids speculative activation |
| Poor access | no relationship path, channel path, event path, or content reach | prevents invisible accounts |
| Procurement-only | buyer only wants lowest-price quote | avoids margin-damaging RFQs |
| Locked supplier | long-term vendor lock with no dissatisfaction | lower priority nurture |
| No committee path | cannot identify or access relevant roles | prevents single-threaded effort |

The group challenges:

- Is the ICP narrow enough for a 90-day pilot?
- What account types should be excluded immediately?
- What signal would prove the segment is moving?
- What buyer roles must be visible?
- What content can be specific only because this ICP is narrow?
- What would sales need to say, "this account is worth action"?
- What would leadership be tempted to broaden too early?

## AI-Assisted Attempt

Ask AI to score the candidate segments.

Required AI output:

| Output | Required Standard |
|---|---|
| Segment ranking | rank by fit, trigger, access, value, and pilot feasibility |
| ICP rationale | explain why the chosen segment is narrow enough |
| Disqualification | list explicit rejection rules |
| Buyer committee | name likely roles for the chosen ICP |
| Demand signals | identify trigger or engagement signals to watch |
| Risks | state why the ICP might still fail |

## Human Correction

Humans must repair AI output when it:

- overvalues market size;
- recommends broad categories without triggers;
- ignores commercial value and sales effort;
- assumes digital access where relationship channels dominate;
- misses distributor, consultant, event, or association access paths;
- fails to include disqualification;
- recommends a segment because it "sounds promising" without evidence.

## RevOps Translation

RevOps must be able to inspect whether account focus is real.

Minimum fields or working columns:

| Field | Purpose |
|---|---|
| ICP fit score | high, medium, low, disqualified |
| Fit reason | why this account belongs or does not belong |
| Trigger type | audit, expansion, new line, quality issue, replacement, tender, supplier risk |
| Trigger evidence | source and date of trigger |
| Segment | pilot segment or excluded segment |
| Access path | sales, event, distributor, association, partner, search, expert profile |
| Disqualification reason | explicit reason for downgrade or exclusion |
| Progression state | Cluster ICP, Future Pipeline, Active Focus, Opportunity |

## Failure Modes

| Failure Mode | What It Looks Like | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Broad market fantasy | "we serve all manufacturers" | narrow by trigger, application, value, and access |
| Logo wish list | target accounts chosen because they are famous | require fit, trigger, proof, and access |
| Price-led inbound trap | RFQs come from weak-fit buyers | define high-fit account and route low-fit inquiries differently |
| No exclusion logic | every account stays in the system | add disqualification reasons and downgrade rules |
| Sales rejection | sales says leads are irrelevant | inspect ICP fit before handoff |
| Leadership scope creep | pilot becomes too broad | protect 90-day focus with stop, repair, scale criteria |

## Final Artifact

Submit an ICP and demand focus statement:

```text
For the next 90 days, we will focus on [category/account type] in [market/geography] where [trigger] creates a need for [operational outcome]. The primary buying committee includes [roles]. We will prioritize accounts with [fit evidence] and exclude [disqualification rules]. We will measure movement through [account progression signals].
```

Also submit a disqualification table with at least five rejection rules.

## Completion Standard

This unit is complete only when the learner can answer:

- why this segment, not the broader market;
- why now;
- why these accounts are commercially worth effort;
- how sales can identify relevance;
- how RevOps will classify fit and trigger evidence;
- what accounts will be excluded.

If the ICP does not change what content, distribution, sales action, or RevOps tracking will do, it fails.
